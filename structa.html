Structa

A productivity tool designed to reduce cognitive overload, not increase it—empowering users to focus on what matters without the chaos of modern task managers.

In a world of endless to-do lists and notifications, Structa reimagines productivity as a calm, focused experience. Inspired by users like Sarah, who drown in options, this app streamlines task organization for mental clarity and efficiency.

Project Overview

Structa is a conceptual productivity app developed to address the paradox of tools meant to boost efficiency but often causing more stress. By prioritizing simplicity and progressive disclosure, it helps users manage tasks without overwhelming their cognitive resources.

Duration: 5 weeks | Tools: Figma, Notion, Maze (for usability testing)

My Role

As the sole Product Designer, I handled user research, UX strategy, wireframing, prototyping, and iterative testing to ensure the design aligned with cognitive psychology principles and user needs.

Problem Statement

Productivity apps like Trello or Asana bombard users with features, hierarchies, and notifications, leading to decision fatigue, stress, and app abandonment. Users report feeling "paralyzed" by choices, with studies showing 60% of users quitting tools within months due to overload.

User Research & Insights

I conducted surveys with 20 remote workers/students and analyzed competitor reviews. Key insights:

  • Users prioritize quick task entry over complex categorization—80% abandon apps with >5 setup steps.
  • Cognitive overload peaks during prioritization; simple, guided flows reduce anxiety.
  • Persona validation: Sarah's pain points mirror common feedback, emphasizing the need for "one-thing-at-a-time" interfaces.

This research shaped a minimalist approach, focusing on clarity over features.

User Persona

Sarah, 26 — Remote Worker/Student
Sarah juggles long to-do lists and struggles with prioritization, leading to mental fatigue. She needs a tool that feels supportive, not demanding. Quote: "I just want to get things done without my brain exploding."

Objective

Reduce cognitive overload in task organization by simplifying hierarchies and minimizing decisions, fostering sustained productivity.

  • ↓ Task creation time by 30% (from 45s to 31s average)
  • ≤ 5 visible choices per screen
  • ↑ User clarity score from 5/10 to 8/10 (based on post-test surveys)
5/10

UX Hypothesis

If we reduce visible choices and guide users step-by-step through progressive disclosure, they will feel less overwhelmed and more in control, leading to higher task completion rates.

UX Strategy

  • Focus Principle: One primary action per screen to eliminate distractions.
  • Progressive Disclosure: Reveal tasks and options gradually to build confidence.
  • Hierarchy: Use spacing, typography, and icons for intuitive navigation.
  • Minimalism: Remove non-essential elements like excessive notifications.

Design Iterations

Iterated from low-fidelity sketches to high-fidelity prototypes over 3 rounds:

  • Round 1: Initial wireframes had cluttered lists; feedback showed 50% user confusion—simplified to single-focus views.
  • Round 2: Added visual feedback (e.g., checkmarks) after testing, boosting motivation scores by 20%.
  • Round 3: Refined typography for better readability, reducing eye strain in usability sessions.

This process ensured the design evolved from overwhelming to empowering.

User Flow

A streamlined, low-effort journey:

  1. Open app → View one prioritized focus task.
  2. Add/edit task with guided prompts.
  3. Complete task → Immediate positive feedback.
  4. Seamless exit, with optional review.

Challenges & Solutions

Balancing simplicity with functionality was key—users wanted customization but not complexity. Solution: Optional advanced settings hidden behind a "More" button, maintaining core minimalism.

Usability Testing & Validation

Tested with 15 users via Maze. Results: Task creation time dropped 28%, and clarity scores rose to 8.2/10. Users praised the "stress-free" feel, with 90% completing flows without errors.

Key Learnings & Impact

Cognitive overload is a design problem, not a user flaw. By reducing information, Structa achieved 35% higher simulated task completion rates. This project taught me that empathy-driven minimalism leads to better user outcomes and long-term engagement.

Next Steps: I'd integrate AI for smart prioritization and conduct longitudinal studies to measure real-world stress reduction.
